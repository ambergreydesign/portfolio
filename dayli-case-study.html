The Heart of It

"I am not a think outside of the box kind of person. I just deal with the inconvenience."

— A neighbor, SAHM to five kids, one of whom is differently-abled

My neighbor Rachel has five kids. One uses a wheelchair and is tube-fed. For years, she dealt with a problem called "feeding the bed" — a faulty connector that causes a mess during tube feedings. It's a widespread problem in the tubie parent community, caused by a lack of competition and innovation in the medical supply market.

One day, another parent told her about a parent who had gotten so frustrated that they designed a better connector and started 3D printing it. They sell it on Etsy. Rachel ordered it. She never had to worry about feeding the bed again.

But here's what stayed with me: Rachel had no idea this existed. She wasn't looking for a solution. She had already accepted the inconvenience. She only found it because she happened to talk to the right person, who happened to know the right person.

Thousands of parents are still feeding the bed right now, not because the solution doesn't exist, but because they haven't met the right person yet.

Why Dayli Exists

Rachel's story isn't exceptional. It's the rule. Most people navigating disability, caregiving, or daily living challenges adapt rather than advocate. They absorb the friction. They just deal with it.

The connection that changed Rachel's life was accidental. Dayli makes it intentional. It connects people like Rachel to innovators like the Etsy parent, at scale, without needing to know the right person first.

My work is about making that system real: designing how people enter it, how the community is structured, and how the platform learns enough about someone's context to surface what's actually relevant to them.

Core Interaction · AI Agent

The Socratic Agent

Instead of asking users to fill out forms or search databases, Dayli uses a Socratic dialogue model: warm, conversational prompts that naturally gather the information needed to surface relevant solutions.

The agent collects context through empathetic follow-up questions: what's difficult, what they've tried, what their situation looks like. No clinical intake forms. No keyword search. Just a conversation that leads to understanding.

Behind the dialogue, the system maps responses to ADL categories (Bathing, Dressing, Mobility, Toileting, Transferring, Eating) and matches against community-sourced solutions, connecting people to answers that came from lived experience.

Two Layers

A structured diagnostic questionnaire captures baseline context (who, what, how much help). Then the Socratic agent refines understanding through open conversation. The combination of structure and warmth is what makes it work.

Dayli AI — Socratic Dialogue
Dayli
Hi! Tell me about a daily activity that's been difficult lately.
User
Bathing has been really hard since my shoulder surgery.
Dayli
I understand — reaching and balancing can be tough after shoulder surgery. Can you tell me more about what specifically is most difficult? Is it getting in and out of the tub, washing certain areas, or something else?
User
Mostly reaching my back and holding the showerhead at the same time.
Dayli
That makes sense. I've found 3 solutions from people in similar situations: a wall-mounted showerhead holder, a long-handle bath sponge, and a shower bench that frees up both hands. Want to explore any of these?
