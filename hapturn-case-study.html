The Problem

GPS is built for people who are already paying attention

Drivers with ADHD and anxiety know their routes. They know where to turn. But between the cognitive load of driving, the lag in audio prompts, and the visual complexity of map interfaces, the moment of decision arrives faster than the cue, and the exit is gone.

The result isn't a navigation failure. It's a feedback design failure. Current GPS systems rely entirely on auditory and visual channels, the same channels already saturated by the demands of driving. For users whose attention regulation works differently, this creates a cascade: a missed exit becomes stress, stress becomes distraction, distraction becomes more missed exits.

The data confirmed what interviews already made clear: the problem isn't the driver. It's the interface. And the interface has room to improve.

Research

What drivers actually said

User research combined quantitative surveys with ethnographic interviews to understand not just the frequency of the problem, but the emotional texture of it. Survey data revealed the correlation between stress and missed exits. Interviews revealed the human cost: the anxiety that compounds every time a prompt arrives too late.

"I am a focused woman,
except in my car."

— Jordan, 28, Software Developer · User Persona

"Yeah, I don't like last minute GPS changes. I don't like that. I wanna know. I need enough time to prepare for that shift."

— Participant 1, Ethnographic Interview

"I feel like I miss turns all the time because I get distracted, and by the time I realize, it's too late. It just adds more stress to an already stressful drive."

— Participant 4, Ethnographic Interview

"I tend to zone out while I am driving to keep calm, so if the directions change I need a heads up. Also the visuals are not always clear. It can be really confusing."

— Participant 2, Ethnographic Interview

Across five interviews, three patterns emerged consistently: the need for earlier notice before turns, frustration with visual complexity during navigation, and a desire for cues that don't compete with the act of driving itself. Stress and missed exits weren't just correlated in the data. They were described as self-reinforcing by nearly every participant.

Define

Two drivers. One shared experience.

Both personas emerged from real research data, not archetypes but composites of actual interview and survey participants. Their situations are different. Their pain is the same.

Persona 01

Jordan

28 · Software Developer · Seattle, WA

"When I'm commuting to work, I'll often miss turns I know well because I zone out. It's frustrating because I feel like I lose focus even on familiar routes, and that just adds more stress to my day."

Jordan is tech-savvy and high-functioning. She manages complex systems at work every day. Behind the wheel, something shifts. She suspects ADHD but is undiagnosed. Her biggest challenge isn't unfamiliar routes. It's the familiar ones that trip her up, because she stops actively paying attention to roads she thinks she knows.

Persona 02

Alex

35 · Graphic Designer · Portland, OR

"Driving in new places is always stressful for me. I rely on GPS, but sometimes the directions are too complicated or come too late, which makes me anxious about missing turns."

Alex is diagnosed with ADHD and describes driving as the only time he feels stressed. Complex GPS prompts overwhelm him. He needs directions that are simpler, earlier, and more frequent. The anxiety isn't about the road. It's about not trusting the system to give him enough time to respond.

How Might We

How might we design tools that provide clear, proactive navigation feedback to help drivers with ADHD stay engaged and reduce stress during drives?

The Solution

HapTurn: navigation through touch

◎

North Star · Focus on Focus

Empower drivers with ADHD and anxiety to navigate with ease using subtle, tactile prompts for turns, lane changes, and exits.

HapTurn is a haptic steering wheel system that delivers tactile feedback for navigation, guiding drivers through subtle vibrations instead of audio prompts that compete with an already demanding environment. No new screen. No new voice. Just a quiet signal through the hands already on the wheel.

By delivering haptic cues for left turns, right turns, lane changes, and exits, and doing so earlier than current systems, HapTurn shifts the navigation experience from reactive to proactive. Drivers with ADHD or anxiety don't have to monitor the system. The system monitors the route for them.

Competitive Landscape

Existing ADAS (Advanced Driver Assistance Systems) use haptic feedback, but only for hazard detection, not navigation assistance. The gap is significant.

Cadillac

Safety Alert Seat delivers directional vibrations (left, right, front, back) to signal detected hazards.

Hazard-only. No navigation prompts.

Audi · Mercedes-Benz

Haptic steering wheel feedback warns for lane drifting and boundary detection.

Lane discipline only. Not directional.

Tesla

Autopilot haptic alerts prompt drivers to regain manual control when necessary.

Automation handoff only. No turn guidance.

The gap: Every existing system uses haptic feedback to alert drivers to danger. None uses it to guide them through a route. HapTurn is the first to apply directional haptics specifically to navigation, designed for the users who need it most.

The Prototype

Built to be felt, not just imagined

The mid-fidelity prototype isn't a clickable mockup. It's a working physical system. Built with a Nano microcontroller, breadboard, cell motors, soldered components, and custom Arduino code, the prototype simulates real haptic feedback patterns through a steering wheel form factor.

Why physical? Haptic feedback can't be fully evaluated on a screen. The point of HapTurn is the sensation itself: how early it arrives, how distinguishable left feels from right, how a sudden route change communicates urgency without panic. That required actually building it.

Testing used a Wizard of Oz methodology. A facilitator triggered vibration patterns manually while participants drove a simulated route, allowing evaluation of timing and clarity without full automation. Iterative adjustments refined delay intervals and motor intensity based on user feedback.

↰

Left Turn

Short pulse on left motor, delivered 400m before the turn. Earlier than standard GPS audio cues.

↱

Right Turn

Short pulse on right motor, same early-delivery principle. Distinguishable by side, not intensity.

⟳

Sudden Route Change

Alternating bilateral pulse pattern, distinct from directional cues to signal urgency without triggering panic.

⇉

Lane Change

Sustained low-intensity vibration on the target-side motor, fading as the lane change completes.

CarPlay Integration

Taking it further, into the dashboard

As part of the development process, I explored what it would look like to integrate HapTurn directly into Apple CarPlay, embedding haptic control into a system millions of drivers already use daily. The interactive prototype below simulates a real navigation drive: as the vehicle approaches a turn, the steering wheel's left or right motor fires early, before the audio cue.

This integration surfaces the broader potential of directional haptics: not as a standalone device, but as a layer on top of existing navigation infrastructure. The cognitive and sensory needs it addresses are widespread. The adoption path should be too.

Try the demo drive, or manually trigger any pattern (left turn, right turn, sudden reroute, lane change) and watch the steering wheel respond. Each pattern is distinct by design: no two situations feel the same.

What I Learned

Six things this project changed about how I design

01

Designing for Neurodiversity

+

Understanding the specific challenges of ADHD and anxiety, like zoning out, stress from missed turns, and cognitive overload, pushed me to think about sensory-driven solutions rather than adding more information to an already saturated interface.

02

Hardware and Software Integration

+

Building the mid-fidelity prototype required calibrating motor intensity and timing with real-time feedback. Working across disciplines like code, circuit design, and UX taught me how to synchronize hardware functionality with actual user needs.

03

Cognitive Load in Design

+

Focusing on simplified navigation prompts and reduced distractions showed me how directly cognitive psychology principles apply to interaction design. Good design for neurodivergent users is almost always better design for everyone.

04

Ethical Research Practice

+

Conducting research with neurodiverse participants required more care than standard user testing. Accommodating individual needs, creating psychologically safe environments, and handling sensitive data with intention shaped how I approach every study since.

05

Iterative Prototyping

+

Overcoming hardware constraints like inconsistent motor response, timing delays, and wiring failures reinforced that the design process doesn't pause when something physical breaks. Adaptability is part of the method, not a deviation from it.

06

Designing Beyond the Screen

+

This project proved that meaningful interaction design doesn't always live on a display. Touch, timing, and spatial awareness can carry just as much information as pixels when the modality matches the moment.

Reflection

Designing for neurodivergence isn't about adding more information. It's about delivering the right information through the right channel at the right moment.
