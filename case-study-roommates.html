Project Overview

A comprehensive UX research investigation into the dynamics of roommate experiences among individuals aged 18-40 in Chicago. Through survey design, data analysis, and qualitative synthesis, the project gathered insights on the common pitfalls of shared living — with the aim of informing the development of an innovative roommate-matching application designed to scale to any city.

The Challenge

The Compatibility Gap

Finding a roommate is rarely about finding someone who can pay rent on time. The real friction lives in the invisible layers — habits, boundaries, cleanliness standards, sleep schedules, and social expectations that only surface after the lease is signed.

Existing platforms treat roommate matching like a transaction: budget, location, move-in date. They filter for logistics but ignore the behavioral dynamics that actually determine whether a living arrangement works or collapses. The result is a cycle of mismatched expectations, unspoken frustrations, and avoidable conflict.

This project asked: what if we could surface the real compatibility signals — the ones people only discover after moving in — and use them to build matches that actually last?

Pain Points

What the data kept surfacing

Across survey responses, four recurring themes emerged — not as isolated complaints, but as systemic patterns in how shared living fails when compatibility is left to chance.

Boundaries

The Invisible Line Problem

Most roommate conflicts trace back to boundaries that were never discussed — because people assume their standards are universal. Cleanliness thresholds, noise tolerance, guest policies, and shared space etiquette all operate on unspoken rules until those rules collide.

The survey revealed that respondents rarely articulated these expectations before moving in. The friction wasn't about bad behavior — it was about mismatched defaults.

Implication: A matching system must surface these invisible expectations before the lease is signed, not after.

Communication

Silence Breeds Resentment

When issues arose, the most common response was avoidance. Respondents described a pattern: irritation builds quietly, passive-aggressive signals replace direct conversation, and by the time the issue is addressed, the relationship is already strained.

The narratives showed that many respondents lacked a framework for raising concerns without escalation. The problem wasn't that they didn't care — it's that they didn't know how to start the conversation.

Implication: The app should include communication resources and strategies for navigating cohabitation challenges.

Lifestyle

Schedules That Don't Align

Sleep schedules, work rhythms, social habits, and domestic routines emerged as primary friction points. An early riser paired with a night owl. A homebody matched with someone who hosts frequently. These aren't personality flaws — they're lifestyle incompatibilities that no traditional listing can predict.

Implication: Lifestyle rhythm matching must be a core component of the algorithm, not a supplementary filter.

Expectations

What "Clean" Really Means

Cleanliness was one of the most frequently cited pain points — but the issue wasn't about hygiene. It was about calibration. One person's "tidy" is another person's "barely acceptable." The gap between individual standards consistently surfaced as a source of low-grade, persistent tension.

Rankings showed that cleanliness norms were among the top three factors respondents wished they'd aligned on before committing to a shared space.

Implication: Matching should use granular behavioral questions, not self-assessments, to calibrate shared expectations.

Strategy
Design Intents

Three principles for the platform

The research didn't just identify problems — it revealed the principles a roommate-matching platform would need to embody to solve them meaningfully.

Transparency
Surface the invisible before it becomes a problem

Every major pain point traced back to an unspoken expectation. The platform must make behavioral standards, lifestyle rhythms, and personal boundaries legible before the match is made — not after the lease is signed.

Compatibility
Match on behavior, not just budget

Existing platforms filter on logistics. This one must match on the behavioral dynamics that determine whether a living arrangement thrives or fractures. Compatibility scores should reflect the full picture — habits, schedules, communication styles, and cleanliness calibration.

Education
Equip people to live well together

Matching is only half the equation. The platform should include resources on communication strategies, boundary-setting, and common cohabitation challenges — turning users into better roommates, not just matched ones.

What Comes Next

Three phases to build the platform

The research opens the door. These three phases map how insights become a product — refining the matching system, validating with real users, and scaling beyond Chicago.

Phase 01
Refine the Algorithm
  • Integrate key survey factors into the matching algorithm
  • Develop visual compatibility scores for intuitive understanding
  • Design a personality quiz tailored to roommate-relevant qualities
  • Build interactive chat features to facilitate pre-match communication
Phase 02
Pilot Testing
  • Test with a diverse user group across Chicago neighborhoods
  • Gather iterative feedback on matching accuracy and user experience
  • Develop in-app education resources on cohabitation strategies
  • Establish feedback mechanisms for continuous improvement
Phase 03
Scale Beyond Chicago
  • Marketing collaborations for broader visibility and reach
  • Ensure accessibility is at the forefront of platform design
  • Adapt matching parameters for different urban contexts
  • Build a sophisticated, user-friendly, and inclusive platform
