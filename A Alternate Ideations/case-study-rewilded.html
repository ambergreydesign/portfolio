Overview

The gap between what zoos say and what they fund.

ReWilded began with a simple observation: zoos describe themselves as conservation organizations that also offer experiences — but structurally, they operate as experience businesses that occasionally fund conservation. That inversion isn't a failure of intention. It's a system operating exactly as designed. The question became: how do you make that visible, and what do you do with it?

The Problem

Zoos are experience businesses wearing a conservation identity.

Most AZA-accredited zoos lead with conservation language — in their mission statements, on their signage, across their marketing. Visitors arrive expecting an institution that prioritizes wildlife protection. What they encounter is an experience designed for family entertainment, with conservation as narrative decoration.

This isn't a criticism. It's a structural observation. Zoos require significant revenue to operate — animal care, habitat maintenance, veterinary services, staffing. But when conservation language is used to justify admission prices and donor giving, while direct conservation funding averages well below 2% of revenue across the sector, a gap opens between what is claimed and what is financially demonstrated. Visitors feel it — they just can't name it.

The dissonance isn't incidental. It's structural — built into the operating model. And structural dissonance produces a specific kind of visitor experience: one where trust slowly erodes without a legible reason. ReWilded was designed to make that structure visible, interpretable, and ultimately designable.

The Research

Financial behavior as a research signal.

IRS Form 990 filings — public records submitted annually by non-profit organizations — contain a layer of information that most visitors never see: where money actually goes. Schedule I tracks domestic grants and conservation giving. Schedule F tracks international conservation activity. Together, they paint a picture of institutional financial behavior that either aligns with or diverges from stated mission.

Across 114 AZA-accredited institutions, three years of data (2019, 2022, 2023) were collected, cleaned, and analyzed. Each zoo was scored on two dimensions: Mission Score — the strength of conservation language in their stated mission — and Action Score — the degree of financial conservation behavior demonstrated through their giving. The gap between the two is the Greenwashing Index (GWI).

GWI = Mission Score − Action Score
Negative = institution does more than it says. Positive = institution claims more than it funds.

The methodology was deliberately observational rather than accusatory. The goal was interpretation, not indictment — understanding what these signals suggest about visitor experience and institutional trust, not assigning moral failure to any individual institution. Systems produce behavior. The research was designed to read the system.

A custom ReWilded Research GPT was built to operationalize this framework — enabling structured analysis of zoo financial data, mission language, and visitor perception signals at scale, using a consistent interpretive methodology across all 114 institutions.

The Tool

See the gap. Read the signal.

The GWI Visualizer maps all 114 institutions across the full index range — from overperformers who give significantly more than their mission language claims, to institutions whose conservation language far outpaces any financial commitment. Select any zoo to see its actual mission statement alongside its 990-sourced financial data and a signal interpretation written in the voice of the research.

Each institution's score is the product of two independent assessments: how strongly the mission statement commits to conservation in language, and how strongly the institution commits in financial behavior. The gap between those two numbers is what visitors feel — even when they can't articulate why.

Use the filter tabs to isolate overperformers, aligned institutions, or those with notable gaps. The color spectrum runs from deep forest green (negative GWI — does more than claimed) through warm amber to terracotta (positive GWI — claims exceed action).

The signal interpretation below each zoo's score translates the financial data into what a visitor is likely to feel — not what an auditor would flag. This is the core ReWilded methodology: reading institutional behavior through the lens of human experience.

The Frameworks

From signal to design.

Identifying the gap was step one. The research was designed to go further — toward a set of frameworks that could translate institutional analysis into design direction. If a zoo scores high on the GWI, what does that actually mean for how the experience should be redesigned? The SIGNS framework answers that question.

Spectacle → Story
Replace performance with narrative
Move from theatrical animal presentation toward honest, keeper-narrated encounters that acknowledge animal behavior on its own terms.
Information → Interaction
Make data participatory
Conservation statistics become meaningful only when visitors can act on them. Static signage becomes real-time giving, tracked publicly.
Gaps → Guidance
Name what's missing
Where the zoo doesn't have answers — about trade-offs, about funding, about captivity — design those absences into honest acknowledgment.
Neglect → Nurture
Surface the invisible labor
Keeper knowledge and daily animal care routines are the most authentic conservation content a zoo has. Most of it goes unseen by visitors.

Alongside SIGNS, the Dissonance Loop provides a framework for how visitors move through moments of institutional friction — and how those moments can be designed to deepen trust rather than erode it.

01
Signal
Discomfort surfaces. Something doesn't add up. The visitor senses a gap between what's been promised and what's present.
02
Containment
The experience holds the discomfort rather than resolving it prematurely. Safety is maintained while the signal is kept alive.
03
Context
Information is offered — not to dismiss the feeling, but to give it shape. What is known. What is uncertain. What is being worked on.
04
Choice
Agency is returned to the visitor. They can go deeper, step back, or redirect. The experience doesn't force a conclusion.
05
Closure
The signal resolves — not into comfort, but into meaning. The visitor leaves with a fuller picture, not a managed one.

What I Learned

What the research taught me about systems and trust.

01

Behavior is the actual message

Visitors don't read 990 filings. But they feel financial priorities through the texture of an experience — what's funded, what's crowded, what's explained and what isn't. Behavioral design starts with institutional behavior, not just interaction design.

02

Dissonance is data

The instinct in design is to smooth friction. ReWilded taught me that unexamined friction is a signal worth preserving. The discomfort visitors feel at a zoo isn't a design failure — it's an honest response to a real tension. The failure is when design covers it rather than interprets it.

03

Scale changes interpretation

Analyzing one zoo tells you something. Analyzing 114 tells you something entirely different — about patterns, outliers, and what "aligned" even means in context. Quantitative research at scale made the qualitative signals legible in a way individual cases couldn't.

Reflection

What ecology already knows, design is still learning to ask.
ReWilded · 2026

Rewilding — in ecology — is the practice of removing human management and letting a system recover its own logic. The same principle applies to institutions. ReWilded asks what happens when you stop managing the narrative and start reading the behavior. The answer, in most cases, is that the truth was already there — in the filings, in the experience gaps, in the discomfort visitors couldn't quite name. The research didn't reveal a problem. It gave the problem a language.
