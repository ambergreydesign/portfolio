Redistributing the AZA

A thought experiment: if every AZA-accredited nonprofit zoo pooled its animals tomorrow — no new land, no new facilities, only the footprints we already have — and resettled every species by the climate, acreage, and social structure it actually needs, what would the map of North America look like? The Premise

Species Sanctuaries

Consolidate each charismatic species into 1–3 dedicated sites chosen for climate, existing infrastructure, and depth of program. Elephants to three sanctuaries. Bonobos to Cincinnati. Orangutans to Indianapolis. Polar bears to Detroit.

What this plan optimizes for Welfare depth, genetic management, and species-level expertise. Every facility keeping that species has the budget, staff, and acreage to do it well — because nothing else competes for those resources on site.
What it trades away Public exposure to variety. A family visit to the Bronx would no longer include an elephant, a rhino, or an orangutan. Some small zoos become native-species conservation centers instead of collections.

    Why Atlanta

    The Gorilla anchor, not the only gorilla site.

    Zoo Atlanta already runs the largest western lowland gorilla program in North America — about 20 individuals across four family troops and a bachelor group, with decades of behavioral research. Its 40 acres aren't enough to be a great zoo at everything, but they are more than enough to be the best zoo at one thing.

    Atlanta cannot hold all ~350 AZA gorillas. It can hold the largest single population — around 30–35 under hybrid logic — and anchor a distributed network of climate-matched satellites. Giraffes, lions, elephants, pandas, and plains animals leave Atlanta entirely, freeing the footprint for gorillas plus the Congo Basin species they would share a forest with.

    Current plan Hybrid — Atlanta ~32 gorillas as the primary site; ~170 more distributed across 6 satellites.

      The honest tradeoff. None of these plans preserves the current ~300–350 zoo-held gorilla population on current AZA footprints. Welfare-first logic consolidates populations to climate-matched sites with deeper programs per individual — and accepts that roughly half to three-quarters of current zoo-held gorillas transition out over ~15 years. That transition is via natural aging, bachelor-group consolidation at primary sites, partnerships with range-country semi-wild reserves (outside AZA), and a slowdown in captive breeding at phased-out sites. The same arithmetic applies to elephants (~100 AZA → ~40 at three reserves) and polar bears (~60 AZA → ~25 at four cold-climate sites). The species decline is not a bug of the plan; it is a feature — the alternative is continuing to hold thinly-distributed welfare-compromised populations at dozens of under-resourced sites.

      Going Deeper

      The species that broke every plan

      Three tests push every model to its limit: great apes (social complexity), elephants (sheer space), and polar bears (thermodynamics). Here's how each plan handles them, and which plan wins for each.

      Great Apes

      Distributed, not consolidated — but only just enough.

      Gorillas, orangutans, bonobos, and chimps each have different answers, and the answer is never one site.

      • Bonobos → 1–2 sites (~100 individuals). Cincinnati dominant (~60), one humid satellite (Audubon or Jacksonville, ~15–25). Bonobos are already rare in AZA — concentration is safe.
      • Gorillas → 6–8 sites (~164 under hybrid, from current ~300). Atlanta primary (~26), satellites at Bronx, SD Safari Park, Disney AK, Cincinnati, Jacksonville, Dallas, Memphis. Silverback family structure means males fight — bachelor groups need separate homes. Population contracts by ~45% over ~15 years.
      • Orangutans → 3 sites (~80 from current ~175). Indianapolis primary (purpose-built canopy center), plus Denver and San Diego. Bornean and Sumatran on different sites — they must not interbreed.
      • Chimps → 3 sites (~150 from current ~260). Lincoln Park Chicago primary (deepest research program), plus Kansas City and North Carolina. Troop fission-fusion behavior only works with 20+ individuals — fewer sites, bigger troops.
      Great apes resist species-sanctuary logic. Their genetics and social structure punish single-site plans. Hybrid wins: a primary center that holds the expertise, and 2–4 satellites that hold the genetic diversity and the family groups the primary can't.
      Elephants

      Three reserves or none at all.

      Wild African elephants walk 25+ miles a day across family herds of 10–20. No urban zoo can approximate this. In AZA's current footprint, only a handful of sites have even the wrong order of magnitude of space.

      • North Carolina Zoo (~2,600 ac) — family herds of African savanna elephants. Climate is humid subtropical, close enough for nine months.
      • San Diego Zoo Safari Park (~1,800 ac) — Mediterranean climate, existing mixed-herd savanna infrastructure, rhino co-grazing already in place.
      • The Wilds, OH (~9,000 ac) — pasture-scale, but cold. Becomes the Asian elephant site rather than African: matches Himalayan foothill seasonality.

      Every other AZA elephant exhibit closes. Individuals are absorbed into family groups at the three reserves — no solitary elephants, no more "pair of two" exhibits. The 30+ zoos that currently hold 1–3 elephants lose them entirely.

      This is the clearest case for species-sanctuary logic. Elephants have already lost the public-facing variety fight; welfare research has made consolidation unavoidable. The plan doesn't create the change — it just finishes it.
      Polar Bears

      Consolidate north, not distribute widely.

      Polar bears require sustained sub-freezing enrichment, deep pools, shaded pack-ice analog, and are increasingly viewed as the species AZA's warmer sites should stop holding. Roughly half of current AZA polar bear sites sit in climates that force constant refrigeration and thermal stress.

      • Detroit Zoo — primary. Already runs the largest polar bear habitat in the US (the Arctic Ring of Life). Climate matches eight months of the year.
      • Minnesota Zoo, Como (St. Paul), Pittsburgh — secondary, each holding a small family unit or bachelor pair.
      • Assiniboine Park Zoo (Winnipeg) — affiliate hub for Arctic rehabilitation, climate-best.

      Warm-climate polar bears (Texas, Florida, southern California) are consolidated north. Their habitats are repurposed for species native to those climates — often native reptiles or amphibian breeding facilities.

      Polar bears prove the climate rule: you can't simulate the Arctic in Houston, no matter the budget. Species-sanctuary wins cleanly here.
      Sanctuary vs Microbiome

      They answer different questions.

      Sanctuaries ask: what does this species need to flourish? One answer, many sites compete to offer it.

      Microbiomes ask: what does this ecosystem need to function? The species list follows — predator, prey, scavenger, pollinator, plant.

      Microbiome thinking is richer for visitors and for cognition: primates with predator cues, prey with predator cues, birds with the insects they eat nearby. It's how Houston already designs its Pantanal and how South African reserves operate.

      But microbiome logic breaks on large-bodied specialists. An African savanna microbiome in a 200-acre zoo still doesn't have enough space for an elephant herd. The ecosystem is right; the scale isn't.

      Microbiomes for cognitive enrichment. Sanctuaries for the species whose needs can't be met inside any 200-acre polygon. Every serious plan is a mix.
