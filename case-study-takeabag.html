Overview

A communal system for what we already throw away.

Despite increasing awareness of the negative environmental impact of single-use plastics, the issue persists due to a lack of accessible, everyday solutions. The result is a continued reliance on single-use bags, contributing to waste and pollution. Take A Bag was designed to address this — not with guilt, but with shared infrastructure that makes sustainable behavior the path of least resistance.

"Sustainability doesn't fail at the point of conviction. It fails at the point of convenience."

The Problem

Awareness without access doesn't change behavior.

People know single-use bags are harmful. The awareness campaigns have worked. But awareness hasn't translated into consistent behavior change — because the friction points are practical, not ideological. Forgetting bags at home, concerns about cleanliness, resistance from store employees, limited availability, inadequate capacity, and storage challenges all conspire against good intentions.

The reusable bag problem isn't a knowledge problem. It's a systems problem — one where individual willpower is expected to override structural inconvenience. Every forgotten bag is a moment where the system fails, not the person. Take A Bag was designed to move the solution from the individual to the infrastructure.

01
Forgetting
The most common failure point — bags left at home, in the car, or at the office despite best intentions.
→ GreenSwap puts bags at the point of need — forgetting becomes irrelevant.
02
Cleanliness
Hygiene concerns about reused bags create psychological barriers to adoption and sharing.
→ Station design uses visual hygiene cues; bags are communal infrastructure, not personal.
03
Resistance
Store employees sometimes push back on reusable bags, adding social friction to an already fragile behavior.
→ Removes the person-to-bag relationship — it's neutral infrastructure, like a shopping cart.
04
Capacity
Limited bag sizes and carrying capacity make reusable bags impractical for larger shopping trips.
→ Multiple sizes at each station. The system scales to the trip, not the other way around.

These pain points aren't edge cases — they're the everyday experience of trying to be sustainable within a system designed for disposability. The question became: what if the solution didn't ask people to change, but changed the environment around them?

The Research

Four lenses on why sustainable behavior breaks down.

The research for Take A Bag moved through four interdisciplinary lenses — each revealing a different layer of why individual behavior change fails to solve systemic environmental problems. Together, they formed the foundation for a design response that works with human behavior rather than against it.

01
Anthropology
Cultural dynamics, social structures, and historical contexts shape attitudes toward sustainable practices. Effective interventions must resonate with diverse cultural backgrounds and acknowledge that sustainability is culturally mediated, not universal.
Design Implication +
GreenSwap removes cultural signaling — it's infrastructure, not activism. The system works without asking people to identify with a cause.
02
Circular Economy
Circular models drive resource efficiency and waste reduction by keeping materials in use. The research explored how communal exchange systems can function as micro-circular economies — extending the life of bags through shared access rather than individual ownership.
Design Implication +
The station network is designed as a micro-loop. Bags stay in circulation rather than ending in disposal — extending life through shared access.
03
Neoliberalism
Free-market ideology places the burden of environmental responsibility on individual consumers rather than systems. This research examined how neoliberal framing turns structural problems into personal failures — and how design can redistribute that responsibility.
Design Implication +
Responsibility shifts to the system. The person just participates — no guilt, no identity politics, no moral ask required.
04
Greenwashing
Corporate sustainability claims often exceed actual environmental commitment, eroding consumer trust and undermining genuine efforts. Understanding greenwashing dynamics was essential to designing a solution grounded in transparency and measurable impact.
Design Implication +
Impact transparency is non-negotiable. The accumulator prototype exists to prove consequence, not persuade belief.
The gap between intention and action isn't moral — it's infrastructural.
Research synthesis — across all four lenses

The Solution

GreenSwap — communal exchange as everyday infrastructure.

GreenSwap is a communal bag exchange system designed to make reusable bag access frictionless. Instead of asking individuals to remember, carry, and manage their own bags, the system creates a shared pool of bags available at the point of need — transforming sustainable behavior from personal discipline into community infrastructure.

Bag Drop Stations

Feature

A user-friendly app providing real-time location tracking of nearby stations, enhancing accessibility and lowering the barrier to both contributing and collecting bags.

Design Principle
Impact Visibility

User Experience

Three flows. One shared system.

The GreenSwap experience is built around three core user types — each engaging with the same infrastructure from a different angle. The system doesn't require all three to be active simultaneously; it's designed so that any single interaction strengthens the communal pool for everyone.

User Flow
Donors
Individuals with surplus reusable bags contribute to the communal pool at designated stations. The act of giving is designed to be as effortless as disposal — no signup, no friction, no judgment. Drop and go.
User Flow
Recipients
Users in need of bags easily locate nearby stations through the app, fostering a sense of community sharing. The experience is designed to feel like access, not charity — normalizing shared resources.
User Flow
Impact
All participants witness the direct effect of their contributions on waste reduction. The system makes collective environmental impact legible — turning individual actions into visible community outcomes.

What I Learned

What this project taught me about designing for behavior.

01

Systems over willpower

Sustainable behavior change doesn't scale through individual motivation. It scales through infrastructure that makes the right choice the easy choice. Designing for communities means designing the environment, not the person.

02

Research shapes the question

The interdisciplinary research didn't just validate the problem — it reframed it. Anthropology, circular economy, neoliberalism, and greenwashing each revealed a different structural layer that individual-focused solutions miss entirely.

03

Visibility creates momentum

When people can see the collective impact of small actions, participation compounds. The interactive map and impact tracking transformed abstract environmental benefit into something tangible — and that visibility became the system's strongest engagement mechanism.

Reflection

The most sustainable behavior is the one that doesn't require you to remember.
Take A Bag · GreenSwap

Take A Bag started with a familiar environmental problem and arrived at a systems-level insight: the gap between knowing and doing isn't a failure of character — it's a failure of design. GreenSwap doesn't ask people to care more. It builds caring into the infrastructure. The bags were always there. The system just needed to make them findable, shareable, and part of something larger than any single trip to the store.
