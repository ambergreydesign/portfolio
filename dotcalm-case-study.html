Project Overview

A research-backed speculative design investigation into what it would take to give children, and the caregivers who support them, a moment before the moment. dotCALM is a two-sided system: a wearable sensory band on the child and a companion app for caregivers. The band detects early signs of overstimulation and initiates a breathing sequence. The app alerts the caregiver and lets them reach across the room, or across the building, with a gentle haptic nudge. Together, they make calm something you can send.

Skills

Designing for the body first: haptic feedback patterns, breathing ring animation, and tactile cues that guide regulation through felt experience rather than instruction.

Designing a two-sided interaction system, the child's wearable and the caregiver's companion app, as a single coherent ecosystem with shared haptic language.

Mid-fidelity wearable prototype plus companion app in Figma with full interaction flows, haptic sequences, and a real-world validation test plan.

Translating caregiver burnout research and sensory processing studies into design constraints that shaped both the wearable and app experience.

Methods & Tools

Literature on sensory processing differences, interoception, and the physiological signatures of pre-escalation states: what the body signals before the mind catches up.

Built a working wearable prototype using an Arduino Nano with haptic motors to simulate the breathing pulse and caregiver nudge patterns, enabling hands-on user testing of the regulation experience before committing to production hardware.

Mid-fi companion app prototype in Figma with full interaction flows from lock screen alert through caregiver response, with neumorphic soft UI and Buzz companion integration.

Designing distinct vibration patterns for breathing cues, caregiver nudges, and escalation alerts so the body can distinguish each signal without visual or auditory input.

The Challenge

The Regulation Gap

Dysregulation doesn't arrive without warning. There are signs: a rise in heart rate, a tightening of breath, a narrowing of focus. These precede the moment the system breaks down. But by the time most support arrives, the window has already closed.

Current support models place the entire burden of co-regulation on caregivers (teachers, parents, aides) who are themselves running on diminishing reserves. The result is a cycle: child dysregulates, caregiver intervenes, both recover, repeat. 57% of surveyed parents reported burnout directly tied to helping children find moments of calm.

This project asked: what if the intervention arrived before the crisis? Quiet, felt, and already underway.

Research Signals

What the research kept saying

Four signals emerged across the research. Not as findings to file away, but as design constraints to work inside of.

Timing

The Window Before the Storm

Every dysregulation event has a pre-escalation phase, a window where the body is already signaling distress before the mind has caught up. Vital signs shift. Heart rate variability drops. The system is loading.

Most interventions target the aftermath. The most impactful design opportunity lives in this window. Quiet, brief, and almost always missed.

Implication: The value of the intervention lives in its timing, not its intensity.

Embodied Learning

Regulation is Physical First

Regulation cannot be instructed into the body. It has to be felt: rhythm, breath, pressure, until the body learns to recognize and recreate that state on its own.

The felt experience is not a supplement to learning. It is the learning. The body's pattern library is written in sensation, not instruction.

Implication: Haptic and tactile cues are not a secondary channel. They are the primary one.

Caregiver Burden

Co-regulation at Scale Breaks

Co-regulation works. But it requires a regulated caregiver, someone with the presence and capacity to hold the child's nervous system long enough to help it settle.

In classrooms of twenty-plus, or homes stretched by work and compounding demands, that capacity runs out. 57% of surveyed parents reported burnout directly tied to co-regulation demands alone.

Implication: The design has to work when caregivers can't be fully present, or can't be there at all.

Autonomy

Independence is the Goal, Not the Shortcut

The goal of any regulation support is internalization, not dependence on the tool. The child eventually recognizes the signals themselves, initiates the response themselves, and no longer needs the prompt.

That's not a product failure. That's the outcome the entire system is optimizing for.

Implication: Success is measured by how quickly the device becomes unnecessary.

The Ecosystem

Two surfaces. One system.

dotCALM is not a single device. It's a shared experience. The wearable belongs to the child. The companion app belongs to the caregiver. They speak to each other through haptic signals that cross rooms, buildings, and the gap between dysregulation and calm.

Buzz · Companion Tools

The Buzz integration is significant. A named companion character gives children an anchor outside of themselves. Feeding Buzz, petting Buzz, playing with Buzz are regulation cues framed as care for something else. The child is practicing self-soothing through the low-stakes act of caring for a companion.

Design Intents

What this system had to do

dotCALM wasn't designed to solve dysregulation. It was designed to reframe where the intervention lives, and who carries the weight of it.

Autonomy
Put regulation in the child's hands

Every design decision pushed toward independence. The band provides scaffolding meant to fade. Buzz gives children a relational anchor that doesn't require a caregiver to be present. The goal is internalization, not dependency.

Timing
Catch it before it breaks

Pre-escalation, not post-crisis. The system catches the rising heart rate, the narrowing tolerance, the quiet signals most environments are too loud to hear. The intervention is already underway before the child needs rescuing.

Embodiment
Less screen. More felt sense.

Haptic feedback and tactile guidance lead. The body doesn't need to be told to calm down. It needs to be shown what calm feels like, again and again, until the pattern becomes its own. The ring expands. The rhythm teaches.

◈
Feature
dotCALM Nudge

When the child needs more than the band can offer alone, dotCALM Nudge allows a caregiver to send a remote haptic signal, from across the room, the building, or the city. A quiet way to say I'm here without interrupting the space, the classroom, or the child's process. It keeps the autonomy intact while preserving the connection that makes regulation feel safe.

What Comes Next

Three phases to test the argument

The prototype opens questions. These three phases are how we answer them, not with assumptions, but with evidence gathered from the people this was designed for.

Phase 01
Refine the Prototype
  • Iterate haptic pulse patterns across different arousal states
  • Test escalation thresholds and cue timing
  • Develop shared haptic vocabulary for the Nudge
  • Refine the Buzz companion mechanic with child users
Phase 02
Define Success
  • Expert interviews to align on behavioral benchmarks
  • Establish measures: independent regulation frequency, time-to-recovery, reduction in caregiver burden over time
Phase 03
Validate in the Field
  • Small pilot with caregivers and educators in classroom and home settings
  • Test cue clarity, classroom feasibility, and perceived child autonomy
  • Iterate based on what the environment teaches us

Reflection

Regulation begins as a feeling. We "Find the pause". That feeling becomes the body's blueprint for self-control and autonomy.
